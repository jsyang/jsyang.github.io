Kaito KA1103 Review
21 / 03 / 2010

Do you have trouble sleeping at night because the government is inside your head? Is the lack of cosmic radiation noise in your life causing your relationships to fail? Well, fear no more! The Kaito KA1103 radio is here to satiate all your shortwave radio fixes! With this tiny, yet feature-rich portable shortwave radio, you too can listen to religious nutjobs, conspiracy theory basketcases and the caffeine-induced rants of local freight truck drivers from the safety of your own home!

I originally bought this shortwave radio in the spring of 2009 after my friend Alex piqued my interest in exploring the airwaves in more detail. Back then, it went for around $100 (shipping included) and I don't think the price has changed much since. You can also get the "non-branded" version of it for about half the price; it moonlights as the Degen 1103. It doesn't come in English but the controls are simple enough to figure out.

The main features of this thing are: SSB (single-side band: lets you listen in on long range local radio transmissions, from trucks and civilian broadcasters), the LCD line panel on the front and the reception qualities.

First the SSB: I've only had enough patience to hone in on a single trucker conversation and that was about a year ago, so without going into much detail, I'm only able to say that the SSB feature of it works as claimed, though actually finding an active transmission can be difficult depending on your location. The LCD front panel is gimmicky as it's basically a hundred segment LCD line display on top of some pre-printed numbers. Literally, all you get is a horizontal line that looks like the analog equivalents for marking where in the spectrum you are when you turn the tuning knob. In that way, it's a bit redundant because right above that, is the more accurate LCD display of the exact frequency you're listening in on. But I guess you could say it looks cool.

On good days (at the proper times) you can listen to worldly radio broadcasts from various other countries in English or otherwise. The included 20 foot wire antenna helps in receiving international broadcasts, but don't expect too much improvement using it for local stations.

The radio also comes with a set of 4 AA Ni-MH batteries that can be set to be charged when you use the AC adaptor. Playback lasts for 5+ hours on a single charge, and I've yet to run them dry from a full charge. A minor flaw is the lack of an overcharge/overdraw circuit in the radio itself so as time goes on, the lifespan of the included batteries might take a hit.

Overall, the radio is a nice handset. An excellent shortwave radio for travel, if you're keen on listening to foreign language broadcasts or seeking between the ticks of a lesser FM radio for weird local stations. Everything a beginner ham needs, in a small and modestly priced package.
