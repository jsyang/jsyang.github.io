Monkeyluv: And Other Essays on Our Lives as Animals ~ Robert Sapolsky
01 / 04 / 2010

ISBN: 978-0-7432-6015-2
Google Books: Summary only, no preview.
Length: 200 pages.
Topics: Ethology, neurobiology, popular science.

The guts: This book is an excellent primer for people especially interested in why animals (people included) do what they do and how the special behavior changes in reaction to circumstance and biology.

Overall: très intéressant!

Monkeyluv is a compilation of published essays by the renowned neuroscientist and primatologist Robert Sapolsky. Ever since sleep-wandering into a heavily wooded area at the tender age of 13, he has a lot of stuff to say on varying interests. These range from misconceptions of the influence of genetics on behavior to brain-altering parasites that can only reproduce in the guts of household cats. In all, this is a wonderful book that warrants the occasional re-reading if your memory is selective FIFO, like mine. Ordering by Amazon marketplace cost me $20 in total, shipping to Canada included.

The book reads like a novel and is split into 4 sections. Each builds up a series of arguments and on the total, growing to higher order biological systems as the left part of the book gets heavier. In sequence, the sections are: "Genes and Who We Are", "Our Bodies and Who We Are", and "Society and Who We Are".

Each section contains about 6 articles narrating contrarian scenarios, based in psychiatry, neurobiology or ethology. Though not particularly in-depth, these tidbits are presented in such a way that you can't help but mark them down later for further Googling. (The emphasis on details and specification really helps here.) Sometimes you need to strategically stop after reading at a point to let your imagination catch up with the words in the book. It's that good.

As an example, "The Pleasure (and Pain) of 'Maybe'", opens with a story that most people can relate to: delusions manifest from social hierarchy and stubborn ignorance. Sapolsky observed Jonathan, a baboon, who, for weeks, exhibited a classic behavior we're all familiar with. Attempting to catch the attention of a particular female, Jonathan used every trick in a lower-ranking baboon's repertoire at every opportunity. Against common logic, the constant rejection should have beaten the possibility of mating out of his small mind. However characteristically, it serves to reinforce his will to continue the petty activities. Believing all the while that they'll lead him to getting some.


A simple tale shows us how even baboons can fall prey to the universal spectacle that statisticians call the Gambler's fallacy and what I call a symptom of mild retardation. In human society, it's absolutely everywhere; it's one of the pathological foundations that give our otherwise silly lives some meaning.
I won't divulge anymore of the article, but little stories like this beg to be followed up if you know what you're looking for. The book is filled with these. A small section of any part of the articles can turn into a huge tangent that will make you hunger for the "why" for days.

It'll make you laugh (at the ridiculous lengths we go to feel "right"), it'll make you cry (for how much you can relate to it); highly recommended!
